Altare della Patria

Altare della Patria, eternal guardian of Italian pride

History and construction

Altare della Patria is a really important symbol of Italy's national identity. It was built to remember the first King of Italy. Also known as the Vittoriano, this amazing building is located in Piazza Venezia, right in the center of Rome. It's big white structure has become a well-known symbol of Italy's history.

1885: Construction started during the reign of Umberto I, and the project was given to the architect Giuseppe Sacconi.

1900–1910: Most of the building was finished, and many Italian artists helped with the decorations.

1911: The monument was officially opened during a ceremony to celebrate the 50th anniversary of Italian unification.

How it is used

Unification Museum: The Risorgimento Museum is inside, where visitors can examine original historical documents.

Panoramic Terrace: In 2007, a panoramic elevator was added, giving an amazing view of the Eternal City.

Official Ceremonies: The monument is also used for national celebrations, and important events are held there regularly.

2023 Restoration: An important project started in March. They had begun cleaning the facade from pollution damage.

Recent facts

April 25 – Liberation Day: This day celebrates Italy’s freedom from Nazi-fascism in 1945. It marks the moment when the National Liberation Committee led an uprising that ended the German occupation and the fascist regime.

June 2 – Republic Day: This day celebrates the start of the Italian Republic, after the 1946 vote when Italians chose to end the monarchy. It’s one of Italy’s biggest celebrations, with events in Rome, including a military parade at the Imperial Forums.

Frecce Tricolori: During both celebrations, the "Frecce Tricolori" (Italy’s aerobatic team) perform spectacular flyovers, leaving trails of green, white, and red smoke.

Legends and fun facts

  • "The Typewriter": The Romans gave it this ironic nickname. The columns look like the keys of a "typewriter".
  • Forbidden Weddings: Wedding ceremonies have been banned. The monument is considered too solemn for weddings.
  • Historical Misunderstanding: The monument has been erroneously associated with fascism, but It was completed before the rise of Mussolini.
