Colosseo

The Flavian Amphitheatre, eternal icon of Rome and masterpiece of Roman engineering

History and construction

The construction of the Colosseum began under Emperor Vespasian in 72 AD and was completed in 80 AD under Titus. It was built on the site of the artificial lake of Nero's Domus Aurea, as a symbol of returning public spaces to the Roman people.

The amphitheater could hold between 50,000 and 87,000 spectators and was used for public spectacles, gladiator fights, and reenactments of famous battles.

How it is used

Today the Colosseum is one of the most visited tourist attractions in the world and an iconic symbol of Rome. In addition to being an open-air museum, it occasionally hosts cultural events and concerts. In 2007, it was included among the New Seven Wonders of the Modern World.

Restoration work continues to preserve this historic monument for future generations.

Recent facts

In 2021, major restoration works on the Colosseum's northern facade were completed, funded by the fashion house Tod's. In 2023, the new underground route opened to the public, allowing visitors to explore the hypogeum.

Recent archaeological studies have also revealed new details about the lifting system used to bring animals and scenery into the arena.

Legends and fun facts

  • It is said that Christian priests used the Colosseum to determine if someone was a vampire
  • The Colosseum had a massive awning (velarium) that could be unfurled to protect spectators from the sun
  • During the inaugural games in 80 AD, the spectacles lasted 100 days
  • Only about one-third of the original Colosseum is estimated to have survived to the present day
