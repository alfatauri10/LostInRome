Piazza del Popolo and The Twin Churches

Piazza del Popolo, where the twin basilicas dance in harmony under the sky of Rome

History and construction

Piazza del Popolo was initially established during Roman times as the starting point of Via Flaminia, the important road connecting Rome to northern Italy, built around 220 AD.

During the reign of Pope Paschal II, around the year 1100, the square gained significance as a key entrance to the city, then known as Porta Flaminia. Its current name derives from the Basilica of Santa Maria del Popolo, constructed with funds donated by the people of Rome (through indulgences).

How it is used

Today, Piazza del Popolo is a vibrant public space, popular with tourists and locals alike. It hosts events, concerts, and celebrations.

Its strategic location makes it an ideal meeting point and a gateway to shopping streets like Via del Corso, drawing visitors to its bars and restaurants.

Recent facts

However, there are no specific details about recent maintenance work on the two twin churches. It is known that over the centuries, both churches have undergone restorations and modifications to preserve their structure and aesthetics.

For example, Santa Maria del Popolo was restored by Gian Lorenzo Bernini between 1655 and 1660, giving it its current Baroque appearance.

Legends and fun facts

  • Not quite twins: Santa Maria dei Miracoli (1681) and Santa Maria in Montesanto (1679) are called “twin churches” due to their similar Baroque style, though they are not identical.
  • The Trident: As mentioned, the square is the starting point of Rome’s “Trident”—three roads (Via del Corso, Via del Babuino, and Via di Ripetta) that fan out like a trident, designed to guide travelers into the heart of the city.
