Ara Pacis

Ara Pacis, where history meets the present and peace becomes a timeless massage.

History and construction

The Ara Pacis in Latin for "Altar of Peace" is an ancient Roman monument dedicated to Pax, the Roman goddess of peace. The Ara Pacis was built between 13 and 9 B.C., during the reign of Augustus. The Ara Pacis was not only an altar, but also a political symbol. Augusto used it to strengthen his image as a bearer of peace and stability, contrasting his government with the chaos of previous civil wars. The altar was part of a larger propaganda campaign, which included the Forum of Augustus and the Mausoleum of Augustus, all designed to celebrate his reign and its benefits.

How it is used

The Ara Pacis is an important symbol of Roman culture and history that continues to have a central role today. Preserved in a modern museum designed by Richard Meier and opened in 2006, it allows visitors to admire the monument up close thanks to its glass structure. It is studied by historians and archaeologists to better understand the age of Augustus and the monument's artistic and political value. The museum also hosts temporary exhibitions, educational activities, and cultural events that connect ancient Roman heritage with contemporary themes. As a popular tourist destination, the Ara Pacis attracts thousands of visitors every year and remains a lasting symbol of peace and prosperity.

Recent facts

Over time, the Ara Pacis has been restored many times to preserve its original details. In the 1930s, Mussolini had ordered its reconstruction as part of his propaganda efforts. In recent years, the monument has been featured in films, documentaries and advertisements. For example, it was shown in the film The Great Beauty (2013). In addition, light projections were used to recreate its original colors, allowing visitors to see how it looked in ancient Rome.

Legends and fun facts

  • It is said that the monument was originally decorated with bright colors, but they have faded over time.
  • Some scholars believe that the reliefs were inspired by Greek art.
  • In the 1930s, it was proposed to move the entire monument to another place, but it was decided to keep it in Rome.
  • The museum is considered controversial because of its modern design, which contrasts with the surrounding historic buildings.
