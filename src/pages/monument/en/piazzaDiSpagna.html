Piazza Di Spagna

Piazza di Spagna, the Roman living room at the foot of the famous Spanish Steps, has enchanted for centuries with its timeless elegance

History and construction

The Barcaccia Fountain was built between 1627 and 1629 during the papacy of Urban VIII Barberini. It was designed by Pietro Bernini and completed with the help of his son, Gian Lorenzo Bernini. The fountain was commissioned to improve the water supply in Rome, utilizing water from the Acquedotto Vergine. Its unique shape, resembling a half-sunken boat, was inspired by a historical event: during the flood of 1598, a boat was said to have been stranded in the square when the Tiber River overflowed.

The fountain was constructed from travertine stone and features the papal coat of arms with sun emblems, a symbol of the Barberini family.

How it is used

Today, the fountain is mainly a popular tourist attraction, visited every day by many people who admire its beauty. It also still serves as a public source of drinking water. On certain occasions, it is also used as a symbolic site for peaceful demonstrations and protests.

Piazza di Spagna also plays an important role in religious traditions: every year on December 8th the ceremony of the floral tribute to the Madonna of the Immaculate Conception takes place, presided over by the Pope. It is a meaningful and well-attended event that draws faithful, authorities, and citizens alike.

Recent facts

The Barcaccia Fountain, located at the center of Piazza di Spagna, underwent a major restoration in 2014 aimed at cleaning and preserving its artistic and architectural structure. However, in February 2015, it was severely damaged by Dutch football fans from Feyenoord who were visiting Rome for a Europa League match. They caused over 110 scratches and significant cracks using glass bottles and firecrackers, even damaging the central candelabra of the fountain, which had been restored and reopened just a few months earlier. The violence led to the arrest of 28 Dutch ultras, and some received sentences of up to four years in prison.

Despite these events, the Barcaccia Fountain remains an iconic symbol of the city and has appeared in several famous films, including *Roman Holiday* starring Audrey Hepburn and Gregory Peck, which contributed to the international fame of Piazza di Spagna.

Legends and fun facts

  • Unlike most fountains, the Barcaccia does not have high water jets because of the low water pressure from the Acquedotto Vergine. For this reason, the fountain is not very large. The strange shape of the boat is thought to be inspired by a "Naumachia," a type of boat used mainly for naval combat.
  • The fountain has been a meeting point for Romans and tourists for centuries.
  • Piazza di Spagna also has a mysterious legend. On autumn nights, the ghost of a woman in a black veil is said to appear. She walks through Trastevere, crosses Ponte Garibaldi, and reaches the Barcaccia fountain, where she suddenly disappears when a voice shouts, "Lorenza!" People say she is Lorenza Feliciani, the wife of Cagliostro, a magician and alchemist. He was allegedly arrested here after she accused him of witchcraft.
